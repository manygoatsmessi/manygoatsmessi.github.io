MISSION LOGOS

ROUND SHAPE

The round design of the logo symbolises the round shape of the planets we leave from to and return from. It also highlights the cylindrical shape of the spacecraft we design.

A

The A represents launch and progress, as well as the first letter of Ares and the two previous human exploration missions (Apollo and Artemis).

RED SIDE

The red side of the logo symbolises our destination, the Red Planet.

BLUE SIDE

The blue side symbolises our home planet, Earth from which we launch. It is also the color of the exhaust of our IPSC fusion engine.

PHASE I

IPSC CONSTRUCTION AND CREW LAUNCH

IPSC CONSTRUCTION

Our most powerful rocket to date, the IPSC Construction Vehicle, will launch the IPSC segments into a Low Earth Orbit roughly 300 kilometers above the surface. Six of such segments will be launched to complete the largest orbiting spacecraft ever constucted.

SUPPLY LAUNCHES

Following the completion of the Interplanetary Spacecraft, numerous supply missions will dock to the station to transfer materials, fuel and life support to the vehicle. These supply modules will later return to Earth.

CREW LAUNCH

Our crew, the final part of the IPSC, will be delivered to the orbiting spacecraft via our Crew Transfer Vehicle. Aboard the station, the crew will be in contact with mission control, ensuring that all systems are ready for deep space travel.

MARS TRANSFER

Once all systems have been checked and confirmed healthy and working, the IPSC will ignite its Zeta-Pinch Torch Fusion Engine to propel the crew away from Earth towards their destination at Mars. They will coast in space for several hundred days before reaching the Red Planet.
PHASE II

LANDING AND BASE CONSTRUCTION

CAPTURE

After over a hundred days in interpanetary space, the crew finally reach their destination. The Interplanetary Spacecraft ignites its engine once again to put itself into Low Mars Orbit. Here, the crew check systems once again to ensure they are configured for landing.

SUPPLY LANDINGS

Before the crew land, supplies will first be delivered down to the landing site. These supplies will house the materials needed to construct the factory on Mars, which will then construct the Crew Colony.

CREW LANDING

The Mars Descent Vehicle will descend from the orbiting IPSC and land at the location of the supplies. As well as the crew, the MDV will house the machines needed to construct the factory. After it is assembled, it will spend over 50 days on the Martian surface printing the parts for the Mars Colony.

SURFACE OPS

Once the colony is assembled, our crew will be able to live inside of it. During the over 100 day stay on the planet, they will perform the first ever crew-conducted science on Mars. Also, they will explore the rim of the landing site crater, as well as the area around it, using the MMSEV.
PHASE III

MAV CONSTRUCTION AND RETURN

MAV CONSTRUCTION

As the end of the surface mission approaches, the astronauts will utilize the factory once again to construct the Mars Ascent Vehicle. Upon completion, it will be moved to Mars Launch Complex 1 in preparation for it to ascend to the IPSC.

LAUNCH TO IPSC

At the optimum time, the MTV will launch to rendezvous with the IPSC. Due to the thinner atmosphere and lower gravity, launching on Mars will be significantly easier than launch here on Earth. It will dock to the IPSC as the crew return to their quarters.

EARTH TRANSFER

The IPSC will, once again, start up its engine and push the spacecraft back to Earth. The journey will span around a hundred days, just like the arrival journey. As it nears Earth, it will use a Lunar Gravity assist to slow the spacecraft down in preparation for the capture burn.

CAPTURE AND REENTRY

After over a year since the first segment launch, the IPSC starts its engine for the final time to put it into a Low Earth Orbit - an orbit just like the one it began with. The crew module will detach and perform its deorbit burn. It will reenter Earths atmosphere at over 20,000 kilometers per hour and splash down in the pacific ocean.
